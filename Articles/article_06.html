THE BEAUTY OF ARCHITECTURE

In every era and civilization, architecture has always been the most telling measure of a society’s level of development. It not only responds to practical functional needs but also embodies the spirit and cognitive boundaries of its time, allowing future generations to glimpse the hopes, pursuits, and wisdom of their predecessors. Throughout humanity’s journey in the vast expanse of cosmic time, monumental architecture evolves with each era, carrying traces of order, will, and creativity. While continually inspiring later generations to explore boldly and pursue hope, it also reflects the ideals and qualities of each period—this is the beauty of architecture.

This is the beauty of architecture.

This is a visual design work exploring the “beauty of architecture,” attempting to narrate the long journey of human civilization from primitiveness to order through minimalist and abstract visual language.

Memory becomes the origin of form.

The work draws its inspiration from fragments of my own memories growing up: as a child, I saw the grandeur of ancient Greek and Roman architecture in textbooks, while in daily life I experienced laborers shaping buildings with the rhythmic strikes of stone; as a teenager, I encountered the fascinating and rhythmic noises of modern machinery and metal clashing at vast concrete construction sites; and during my later travels, I discovered the unique beauty of diverse architectural forms. All these sensory memories and experiences became the starting point for my visual creation.

Some of the sound materials in the video were sampled years ago, when I casually recorded the sounds of construction sites with my phone while passing by. These “noises” became an abstract component of the work, adding a tangible sense of reality and vitality to the visuals.

The work adopts blue as its overall tonal base—symbolizing the origin of life, the ocean, and the emergence of consciousness. Against this pure and profound background, geometric forms gradually appear, beginning with primitive vertical structures that metaphorically represent humanity’s initial understanding of nature and instinctive exploration of “structure” in the untamed era. As the forms evolve, these simple volumes become increasingly regular and proportioned, symbolizing the Stone Age, when humans began to master tools and establish order.

As development continues, the forms gradually move toward classical order, alluding to the emergence of columns and concrete in ancient Greek and Roman architecture—marking one of the earliest systematic explorations of beauty, proportion, and material application in architectural history, and reflecting humanity’s transition from mere survival to aesthetic pursuit. Ultimately, these vertical structures are deconstructed and reassembled into the dense skyline of modern cities, transforming into reinforced concrete skyscrapers that symbolize industrial civilization’s mastery of steel, glass, and concrete, as well as the advanced development of contemporary society.

From chaos → order · From nature → reason · From classical → modern

The entire visual process moves from “chaos” to “order,” from “nature” to “rationality,” and from “classical” to “modern.” Rather than depicting specific buildings, the work uses abstract formal evolution to distill a more essential meaning of architecture—that is, humanity’s continuous exploration of space, structure, beauty, and materials, as well as the enduring upward projection of civilization’s spirit.
