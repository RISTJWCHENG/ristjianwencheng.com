Walking Through the Layers of Brussels, Belgium

2022
2 min read

Rationality, institutions, order, negotiation, decision-making—when these grand narratives unfold, what people first perceive is the outline of power and well-ordered structure. Together, they shape the external imagination of this city: it is precisely Belgium's capital, Brussels, and also the "capital" of the European Union in both political and geographical senses.

When I walk through this city, in those wonderful moments—sunlight pouring down on a summer afternoon—I move among the lines of sculptures, the textures of marble, and the domes of buildings, feeling the past glory and the wisdom of civilization, while harboring endless respect for those fearless pioneers and tireless creators.

However, as residents, people are often more attuned to the city's multiple facets. Power and everyday life coexist, reason and sensibility clash; institutions can be both rigidly structured and, at times, inefficient or loosely organized. Acknowledging this complexity does not mean avoiding the problems. On the contrary, candidly reflecting on the city's "less-than-ideal" aspects should not be overshadowed by the halo of being the "Capital of Europe." Such reflection is a form of self-correction and a prerequisite for maintaining the city's vitality.

For travelers, however, another perspective emerges: every street and alleyway exudes its own unique charm. Belgium is renowned for its beer—the golden liquid shimmering in glasses, foam gently rolling, carrying centuries of brewing wisdom. From the dark, rich abbey beers to the crisp lagers of corner pubs, every sip is a continuation of history and storytelling. Cafés fill the air with the rich aroma of coffee, like a warm mist gently embracing every passerby, while the city's signature chocolate releases deep cocoa scents, its sweetness and richness intertwining, letting time flow slowly across the tongue, inviting one to pause. Colorful comics leap from walls, narrating tales of humor and creativity. The Grand Place, centered around the magnificent Town Hall and surrounded by historic buildings, unfolds like an open-air gallery: sculpted façades and spires glimmer under golden sunlight, echoing past wealth and splendor; the cobblestones reflect light and shadow, letting history and laughter intermingle in the air. Every step reveals the city's glory and the weight of time. Classical carvings juxtapose with modern glass façades, church domes shimmer in shifting light, and streets, coffee aromas, beer foam, and comic colors converge, composing the unique warmth and rhythm of Brussels.

When you walk through the city, your gaze follows the tram tracks stretching toward the street's end, where the 19th-century Roman-Byzantine Église Royale Sainte-Marie rises prominently. Its dome solemnly and silently watches over the city, serving not only as a religious symbol but also as a witness to historical brilliance—reminding modern observers that past courage and creativity forged the enduring, and inspiring future generations to pursue higher values and achievements with passion and wisdom.

What we face is not the city itself, but our own stance and actions. Just as with any topic of discussion, we need both reason—to analyze structures and logic—and sensibility—to perceive what is worthy of appreciation. The city's multiplicity cannot be erased, and its contradictions and complexities cannot be entirely resolved. Yet we can choose where to linger, where to focus our attention, and how to respond through action to the values we recognize. As modern citizens, we are not merely shaped by our environment; we are agents capable of imbuing it with meaning.

We are not mere spectators. A city is not an abstract political symbol, but a living community composed of countless individuals. The civility of public spaces, the conscious respect for rules, and the ongoing pursuit of technological and cultural progress all depend on stable and sustained participation from individuals. While institutional design is important, without everyday, subtle civic practices, even the grandest structures cannot truly take root. A single act of following rules, a moment of respect toward others, a participation in public affairs—each is a concrete contribution to the city's quality. In other words, the city's character arises both from macro-level structures and from micro-level individual actions. Each person is part of the city, and each is a shaper of its future form.
